What Is ASC 946 and Why Does It Matter?
Background, significance of the determination, and the two-tier framework
ASC 946, Financial Services — Investment Companies, provides the accounting and financial reporting model for entities classified as investment companies. The determination is binary and consequential: an entity is either an investment company subject to ASC 946, or it is not, and the accounting treatment is fundamentally different between those two worlds.
Why the Determination Matters So Much

Investment companies under ASC 946 carry substantially all of their investments at fair value through net income — changes in fair value flow directly to earnings regardless of whether they are realized. Non-investment companies follow the specific GAAP guidance for each asset type they hold (e.g., equity method for significant influence investments, historical cost for certain securities, ASC 350 for intangibles). Getting the classification wrong produces dramatically misstated financial statements.

If Entity IS an Investment Company (ASC 946)
  • Investments measured at fair value through net income
  • Specialized disclosures (financial highlights, schedules of investments)
  • No consolidation of investees — held as investments at FV
  • Performance measured by total return (income + appreciation)
  • Financial statements: statement of assets & liabilities, statement of operations, statement of changes in net assets
If Entity is NOT an Investment Company
  • Each asset follows its applicable GAAP standard
  • Consolidation required when controlling interest held
  • Equity method for 20–50% ownership
  • Historical cost (less impairment) for many assets
  • Standard financial statements: balance sheet, income statement, cash flows
  • Crypto assets: currently ASC 350 (intangible asset) or, if elected, ASC 321 FV option
The assessment framework was significantly revised by ASU 2013-08, which replaced the previous rules-based approach with a principles-based, characteristics-focused model. The current framework requires evaluating two Fundamental Characteristics (both must be present) and six Typical Characteristics (evidence of investment company status — not all required, but more is stronger evidence).
Regulated Investment Companies — Automatic Qualification

Entities regulated under the Investment Company Act of 1940 (mutual funds, ETFs, closed-end funds, BDCs registered under Section 8) are automatically investment companies under ASC 946 — no further assessment is required. This practice aid is primarily relevant for unregistered entities where the determination requires judgment: private funds, crypto funds, digital asset managers, DAOs with fund-like structures, family offices, holding companies, and similar entities.

Fundamental Characteristics — Both Required
ASC 946-10-15-12 through 15-14. Both must be present; absence of either means the entity is NOT an investment company.
Critical Rule

The Fundamental Characteristics are threshold conditions — they are not a matter of weighing or balancing. If the entity does not have both characteristics, it cannot be an investment company under ASC 946, regardless of how many Typical Characteristics it exhibits. The assessment stops here if either is absent.

FC-1
Investment Purpose: The entity's business purpose is to invest for returns from capital appreciation, investment income, or both
▶
The entity must exist for the purpose of investing — that is, its primary reason for being is to deploy capital into financial instruments, other assets, or interests in other entities, and to generate returns for its investors through appreciation, income distributions, or both. This is a purpose test, not merely an activity test.
The purpose must be evident in the entity's governing documents, offering memoranda, investor communications, management representations, and actual activities. An entity that holds investments as a byproduct of its operational activities — such as a technology company that receives warrants from customers — fails this test even if it holds many appreciated investments.
The distinction between "investment for return" vs. "operational strategic purpose" is the most common area of judgment. A parent company that holds subsidiaries to operate them strategically (not to realize investment gains) is not an investment company. A fund that holds those same subsidiaries with the primary intent of selling them at a gain is.
✓ Supports FC-1
  • Governing documents state investment/return generation as primary purpose
  • Defined investment period and liquidation/exit strategy
  • Performance measured by IRR, MOIC, or total return
  • Management evaluated on investment returns
  • Exit/sale is the expected monetization event
  • No operational involvement in investees
  • Carried interest or performance fee structure
✗ Undermines FC-1
  • Entity holds assets for strategic, operational, or synergistic reasons
  • Entity operates portfolio companies (management involvement)
  • Investments are not expected to be sold for gain
  • Business purpose is a product/service, not return generation
  • No defined fund life or exit strategy
  • Management evaluated on operational metrics, not investment returns
🪙 Digital Asset Application — FC-1

Crypto fund / digital asset fund: If the entity's documents state its purpose is to generate returns for investors through investment in crypto assets (BTC, ETH, altcoins, DeFi protocols, NFTs), FC-1 is clearly met. The investment return purpose is explicit.

Crypto operating company holding treasury crypto: A company whose primary business is providing crypto exchange, custody, lending, or blockchain infrastructure services — but which also holds crypto on its own balance sheet for treasury purposes — does NOT meet FC-1. The treasury position is incidental to operations.

Entity holding crypto "on behalf of investors" for yield: If the entity's structure and purpose is to pool investor capital into crypto assets to generate yield (staking rewards, lending interest, liquidity provision fees), FC-1 is likely met — provided this is the primary purpose, not a side activity. The key risk is whether the entity has also taken on operational characteristics (running validators, operating DeFi protocols) that undermine the pure investment purpose.

Staking-as-service provider vs. staking-as-investor: An entity that operates staking infrastructure as a service for third parties (a validator operator) is acting operationally. An entity that delegates its investors' crypto to validators to earn yield is acting as an investor. Delegating = investment activity. Running validator nodes for others = operational activity.

FC-1 Assessment
FC-2
The entity obtains funds from one or more investors for the purpose of providing those investors with investment management services
▶
The entity must be in the business of managing investments on behalf of others. It receives funds from investors with the understanding that the entity will manage those funds as investments and deliver investment returns. This characteristic distinguishes an investment company from a company that simply is an investment (e.g., a portfolio company).
"Investment management services" means the entity actively manages the portfolio — making or participating in investment decisions, asset allocation, security selection, risk management — rather than passively holding assets. Importantly, a single-investor fund can still meet this criterion: the investor count requirement is in the Typical Characteristics (TC-2), not here.
The "for the purpose of providing investment management services" language is critical. If investors contribute capital and simply receive a pro-rata share of whatever the entity earns from its operations — with no active investment management occurring — this characteristic may not be met. The entity must actually be performing investment management.
✓ Supports FC-2
  • Entity has a defined investment manager (GP, advisor, manager)
  • Investment management agreement in place
  • Capital contributed by LPs / investors for investment purposes
  • Management fee charged to investors for advisory services
  • Investment policy statement / mandate governs decisions
  • Investors are passive (no day-to-day operational role)
  • Entity reports performance to investors (capital statements, NAV)
✗ Undermines FC-2
  • No defined investment manager — entity is self-managed operationally
  • Investors are also operators (co-management by all investors)
  • Capital obtained for operational business purposes, not investment mgmt
  • Entity simply operates a business; investors receive operating returns
  • No investment mandate or portfolio management framework
  • No management fee — returns are purely from operations
🪙 Digital Asset Application — FC-2

Crypto fund with a manager: A fund with a GP/manager that selects and manages a portfolio of crypto assets on behalf of LPs clearly provides investment management services. FC-2 is met.

DAO / decentralized protocol: DAOs present significant complexity. If token holders vote on protocol direction and the "fund" is essentially self-governed by participants, the question is whether there is a distinct investment management function. A DAO that collectively decides how to deploy treasury funds may not have a sufficiently distinct "manager" providing investment management services. This is an area of significant judgment.

Crypto yield platform (e.g., pooling investor assets for staking): If the platform takes custody of investor assets, makes decisions about where/how to deploy them (which validators, which protocols), and charges a management or platform fee, this looks more like investment management. If investors retain direct control and the platform merely facilitates, the analysis is less clear.

Staking consideration: If the entity pools investor crypto and makes decisions about delegation — which validators to use, when to move between validators, how to optimize yield — that decision-making function is analogous to investment management. If the entity simply routes assets to a predetermined validator with no discretion, the investment management element is weaker.

FC-2 Assessment

Typical Characteristics — Evidence Assessment
ASC 946-10-15-12(b). Six characteristics assessed holistically — not all required, but more present = stronger evidence of investment company status.
How to Read the Typical Characteristics

The typical characteristics are not individually required — they are evidence points. The ASC 946 framework asks whether the entity's facts and circumstances, taken as a whole, indicate it is operating as an investment company. All six present = very strong evidence. Four or five present, depending on which ones = likely. Two or three = judgment-intensive. One or fewer = likely not an investment company even if both fundamental characteristics are met.

FASB indicated that TC-6 (Fair Value Basis) is the most critical of the six — an entity that does not manage its investments primarily on a fair value basis is unlikely to be an investment company regardless of other characteristics.

TC-1
More than one investment
▶
Investment companies typically hold portfolios of multiple investments to spread risk and generate diversified returns. Holding a single investment is not disqualifying — single-purpose vehicles exist — but it is a weaker indicator. An entity holding one investment should have a compelling explanation for why it otherwise qualifies.
How to count: Each discrete investment (position in a security, loan to a different borrower, interest in a different real estate property) counts separately. However, multiple positions in the same issuer may be treated as one investment in some contexts.
🪙 Digital Asset Application — TC-1

A crypto fund holding BTC, ETH, SOL, and positions in DeFi protocols clearly has multiple investments. A fund holding only BTC (e.g., a Bitcoin-only fund) technically has one investment — but this single-asset concentration is common in crypto funds and, by itself, is not disqualifying. Consider whether the fund holds BTC across multiple custodians or structures that might be treated as multiple positions. Note that a Bitcoin ETF, despite holding only BTC, is clearly an investment company — the single-asset characteristic is outweighed by all other evidence.

For staking vehicles: a single-asset staking fund (ETH-only staking) has one "investment" but generates income through a well-defined investment activity. This characteristic alone does not disqualify it.

TC-1 Assessment
TC-2
More than one investor
▶
Most investment companies pool capital from multiple investors. However, single-investor funds (managed accounts, separately managed accounts, feeder funds) can still be investment companies — this characteristic is evidence, not a requirement. A single-investor vehicle managed by a third-party investment manager under an investment mandate has strong evidence on other characteristics that can outweigh the single-investor limitation.
What counts as a separate investor: Each entity or person with a distinct ownership interest counts. Feeder funds that consolidate their interest count as one investor from the master fund's perspective, but the master fund may still qualify as an investment company.
🪙 Digital Asset Application — TC-2

Most crypto funds and yield-generating crypto vehicles have multiple investors by design. A pooled staking vehicle where many token holders deposit ETH in exchange for liquid staking tokens (like stETH) has many investors and clearly meets TC-2. A family office that self-manages its own crypto portfolio has only one "investor" (itself) — this characteristic is not met, but the entity may still fail for other reasons (typically FC-2 as well).

TC-2 Assessment
TC-3
Investors that are not related parties of the investment manager or general partner
▶
This characteristic tests whether the investment management function is genuinely independent — i.e., the manager is managing money for unrelated investors at arm's length, not simply organizing family wealth or creating a structure between affiliated entities. When investors are all related to the manager, the arrangement resembles a holding company more than a fund.
Related parties for this purpose generally include family members, affiliates, entities under common control, and employees of the investment manager. If all capital comes from related parties, this characteristic is not met. If a mix of related and unrelated investors participate, partial credit may be appropriate — but consider whether the unrelated investors are incidental or central to the entity's character.
🪙 Digital Asset Application — TC-3

A public staking protocol or crypto fund open to any investor clearly has unrelated investors. The challenge arises with early-stage crypto funds or DAOs where the founders, developers, and initial token holders are closely affiliated — if the initial investors are predominantly team members and affiliates, TC-3 may not be met initially, even if the intent is to open to the public. Consider evaluating at the reporting date, not at inception.

TC-3 Assessment
TC-4
Ownership interests are in the form of equity or partnership interests
▶
Investors in an investment company typically hold equity-type interests — limited partnership interests, shares of common stock, membership interests — that represent a residual claim on the net assets of the fund. This is the classic LP/GP or fund share structure. The ownership interest should represent participation in the gains and losses of the investment portfolio, not a fixed return.
Entities where "investors" hold debt instruments (notes, bonds) that pay a fixed return regardless of investment performance are not exhibiting this characteristic — those holders are creditors, not investors. However, participating notes or profit-participation arrangements that share in gains might be closer to equity-like interests depending on the specific terms.
🪙 Digital Asset Application — TC-4

Fund interests (LP units, LLC membership interests): Classic investment company structure — TC-4 clearly met.

Liquid staking tokens (LSTs): Tokens like stETH, rETH, or cbETH represent a pro-rata share of pooled staked ETH plus accumulated rewards. These function economically like equity interests — each token represents a residual claim on a proportionate share of the pool. Strong argument that LSTs satisfy TC-4.

Yield-bearing tokens vs. fixed-return tokens: If a token pays a variable return based on staking yields (analogous to dividends on equity), it looks like an equity interest. If a token pays a fixed APY regardless of underlying performance (structured like debt), TC-4 is less clear.

Governance tokens with economic rights: Governance tokens that also carry proportionate economic interests in protocol revenue may qualify, but this requires careful analysis of the token's specific rights.

TC-4 Assessment
TC-5
Pooling of funds
▶
Investment companies typically pool the capital of multiple investors into a common fund, which is then invested collectively. Pooling provides economies of scale (access to investment opportunities too large for any single investor), diversification, and professional management. The pooled nature means no single investor controls the investment decisions of the fund.
A separately managed account (SMA) where a single investor's assets are kept segregated and managed specifically for that investor is not pooled. Such arrangements may still qualify as investment companies under certain circumstances, but TC-5 would not be met.
🪙 Digital Asset Application — TC-5

Pooled staking protocols: The entire value proposition of liquid staking protocols (Lido, Rocket Pool, Coinbase cbETH, etc.) is pooling — individual ETH holders who don't meet the 32 ETH validator minimum contribute to a shared pool. This is textbook pooling. TC-5 strongly met for pooled staking vehicles.

Validator node operated for individual account: An entity running a single dedicated validator for its own account (or one investor's account) is not pooling. TC-5 not met in this structure.

DeFi liquidity pools: Liquidity pools (Uniswap, Curve, Aave) pool capital from multiple participants — in a mechanical sense, this resembles pooling. However, the purpose is operational (facilitating trades, lending) rather than investment management, which affects the FC analysis more than TC-5.

TC-5 Assessment
TC-6
Reporting entity manages substantially all of its investments on a fair value basis (Most Critical)
▶
FASB's Most Critical Characteristic

FASB explicitly noted in the basis for conclusions of ASU 2013-08 that TC-6 carries the most weight among the typical characteristics. Managing investments on a fair value basis is the hallmark of investment company accounting — it is the reason the ASC 946 model exists. An entity that primarily measures and manages on a cost or other basis is unlikely to be an investment company regardless of how many other TCs are present.

"Manages on a fair value basis" means the entity actually uses fair value information to make investment decisions — entry decisions, exit decisions, portfolio monitoring, and performance measurement all rely on fair value. This is distinct from simply reporting at fair value: an entity could be required to report at FV for accounting purposes without actually managing its portfolio on that basis.
Evidence of fair value management includes: investment committee materials based on FV/NAV, investor reporting showing FV, performance metrics based on total return (appreciation + income), formal valuation processes for illiquid investments, and management represented by a portfolio management function rather than a board overseeing operations.
✓ Supports TC-6
  • Investment committee uses FV data to make buy/sell decisions
  • Investor capital statements show FV of portfolio
  • Performance reported as total return (income + appreciation)
  • Formal quarterly/annual valuation process for illiquid assets
  • NAV-based investor entry/exit pricing
  • Portfolio monitoring on mark-to-market basis
  • Risk management focused on FV exposure
✗ Undermines TC-6
  • Investments carried at cost; FV not tracked internally
  • Performance measured by operating metrics (revenue, EBITDA)
  • No formal valuation process; assets not marked to market
  • Management decisions based on operational considerations, not FV
  • Investor returns are fixed/contractual, not FV-based
  • Acquisitions evaluated on strategic fit, not FV IRR
🪙 Digital Asset Application — TC-6

Crypto funds: Most crypto assets trade on active markets with readily determinable fair values (and most are now required to be measured at FV under ASU 2023-08 for non-investment-company entities anyway). Crypto fund managers unambiguously manage on a FV basis — entry/exit decisions, position sizing, and risk management all use real-time market prices. TC-6 is typically the strongest characteristic for crypto funds.

Staking vehicles: The value of staked ETH (or other PoS assets) is determinable by reference to the market price of the underlying token. A staking vehicle that tracks the fair value of its staked position and reports NAV to investors manages on a FV basis. TC-6 met for well-structured staking vehicles.

Key distinction: If the staking vehicle focuses only on the yield rate (APY) and not on the fair value of the underlying staked principal, this may suggest the entity is managing on a yield/income basis rather than a total-return/fair-value basis. Comprehensive FV management requires tracking both income and the mark-to-market of the principal position.

Illiquid tokens / early-stage investments: Entities holding illiquid tokens or early-stage investments need a formal valuation process. If the entity uses ad-hoc or no valuation methodology for illiquid positions, TC-6 is weakened even if liquid positions are marked to market.

Digital Asset Entity Context — How the Framework Applies
Detailed analysis of how ASC 946 criteria apply across common digital asset entity structures
The digital asset space has produced a wide variety of entity structures — many of which sit in genuine grey areas with respect to the ASC 946 determination. The core challenge is that the traditional markers of investment vs. operational activity are blurred in crypto: a company can "hold crypto" that is simultaneously an investment and an operational tool; staking generates income that looks like investment income but requires operational infrastructure; DeFi protocols pool capital like funds but operate like software platforms.
The Central Question for Any Digital Asset Entity

Is the entity deploying capital in crypto assets to generate investment returns for investors, or is it operating a business that happens to involve crypto? If you can describe the entity's primary value proposition without mentioning investors' return expectations — i.e., if the core product is an exchange, a wallet, a protocol, or infrastructure — it is probably not an investment company regardless of what it holds.

The "Incidental Activities" Issue

ASC 946-10-15 notes that investment companies may have activities that are incidental to their investment activities without losing investment company status. The question is whether operational activities (validator operations, protocol governance, treasury management) are truly incidental to the investment purpose, or whether they represent a distinct and material operating business. There is no bright-line test. Consider: if the operational activities were stripped away, would the entity still function as an investment company? If yes, the operational activities may be incidental.

Staking, Delegating & Validating — Impact on the Assessment
Detailed analysis of how different staking structures affect each ASC 946 criterion
Staking activities sit at the intersection of investment and operational activity, which is exactly why they complicate the ASC 946 analysis. The way the entity participates in staking — whether passively delegating, actively running validator infrastructure, or operating as a pooled staking protocol — has significant implications for each criterion.
Understanding the Staking Spectrum

Proof-of-stake blockchains (Ethereum, Solana, Cosmos, Cardano, Avalanche, etc.) require participants to lock tokens as economic security. Returns are generated through block rewards and transaction fees. The key distinction for ASC 946 is who bears the operational responsibility and risk:

  • Delegating: Token holder assigns their staking rights to a validator and receives a share of rewards. Passive — analogous to lending securities or investing in a bond for yield.
  • Validating: Token holder or entity runs the actual validator node — uptime monitoring, software updates, slashing risk management. Active infrastructure operation.
  • Pooled staking (LSTs): Many holders contribute to a pooled protocol that collectively stakes and issues representative tokens. The protocol operates the validators; holders are passive.

Passive Delegation

  • Holder selects a validator; tokens delegated
  • No infrastructure to run or maintain
  • Income = staking yield (investment income)
  • Slashing risk: limited (delegator vs. validator)
  • Analogous to: investing in a bond/money market fund
  • ASC 946 view: Supports investment company characterization — clearly an investment activity generating income from deployed capital

Active Validation

  • Entity operates validator node(s)
  • Infrastructure, uptime, software management required
  • Income = block rewards + transaction fees + MEV
  • Full slashing risk on operator
  • Analogous to: operating a financial services business
  • ASC 946 view: Operational activity. Running validators for one's own return is less investment-like. Running for others' return looks more like a service business. Undermines FC-1 and TC-6.

Pooled Liquid Staking

  • Protocol pools deposits; issues liquid staking tokens
  • Protocol operates validators; depositors are passive
  • LST = proportionate claim on pool + rewards
  • Secondary market liquidity for the LST
  • Analogous to: ETF or money market fund
  • ASC 946 view: Most investment-company-like structure. FC-1, FC-2, TC-2, TC-4, TC-5, TC-6 all potentially met. The protocol issuing LSTs may be an investment company; the protocol operating infrastructure may be an operating company. They are often separate legal entities.

The Slashing Risk / Operational Risk Issue

When an entity runs validator nodes, it bears slashing risk — the blockchain protocol can penalize (slash) the validator's staked balance for downtime or malicious behavior. From an ASC 946 perspective, this is significant: slashing risk is an operational risk (risk of loss from infrastructure failure or misconfiguration), not a market risk or credit risk. An entity bearing full operational slashing risk as a validator is exhibiting characteristics of an operating company, not an investment company.

Contrast this with delegators, who may bear limited indirect slashing exposure but do not bear the operational responsibility. This distinction is analogous to the difference between a fund that invests in bank loans (bears credit risk — investment risk) vs. a bank that originates loans (bears operational and credit risk — operating company).

MEV (Maximal Extractable Value) and Its Impact

Entities running validators may earn MEV — income generated by reordering, inserting, or censoring transactions within blocks. MEV is fundamentally an operational revenue stream derived from running infrastructure, not from investment activity. An entity whose material revenue is derived from MEV extraction is exhibiting operational company characteristics. The larger MEV is as a proportion of total income, the more it undercuts an investment company argument.

Restaking (EigenLayer and Similar Protocols)

Restaking protocols (like EigenLayer on Ethereum) allow already-staked assets to be simultaneously staked for additional protocols, earning additional yield. From an ASC 946 perspective, restaking is an additional layer of investment activity (deploying staked capital for additional return) — it supports the investment company characterization if done passively through a restaking manager. However, entities that operate Actively Validated Services (AVSs) on restaking platforms are again performing operational activities, not purely investment activities. The same delegation vs. operation distinction applies.

Illustrative Scenarios
Walk-through examples applying the framework to common digital asset situations
Scenario 1
CryptoAlpha Fund, LP — A traditional crypto hedge fund structure
▶
Facts

CryptoAlpha Fund, LP is a Cayman-exempted limited partnership with a Delaware-based general partner. The GP charges a 2% management fee and 20% carried interest. The fund has 45 unrelated LP investors who contributed $250M in capital. The GP invests in BTC, ETH, liquid tokens, DeFi protocol tokens, and early-stage SAFT agreements. The fund operates a 5-year life. All investments are tracked on a fair value basis, and monthly NAV reports are sent to investors. The fund earns staking yield by delegating ETH to third-party validators.

Assessment — Meets ASC 946
  • FC-1: ✓ Explicit investment purpose — governing docs, investment mandate, defined fund life, carried interest structure
  • FC-2: ✓ GP provides investment management services to LP investors; management fee paid for advisory
  • TC-1: ✓ Multiple investments across multiple tokens
  • TC-2: ✓ 45 LP investors
  • TC-3: ✓ LPs are unrelated third parties
  • TC-4: ✓ LP interests = equity interests in the fund
  • TC-5: ✓ Pooled capital structure
  • TC-6: ✓ All investments tracked at FV, NAV reported monthly
Staking Note

The fund delegates ETH to third-party validators — this is passive investment income and does not undermine the investment company conclusion. The staking activity is incidental to and consistent with the investment purpose. Staking rewards are investment income analogous to dividends. The fund does not operate the validator infrastructure.

Conclusion: Investment Company under ASC 946 — All criteria clearly met. Investments carried at fair value through income.
Scenario 2
StakePool Protocol LLC — A pooled ETH liquid staking platform
▶
Facts

StakePool is a protocol that accepts ETH deposits from retail and institutional users and issues liquid staking tokens (spETH) representing each depositor's pro-rata interest in the pool plus accumulated staking rewards. StakePool's smart contract allocates pooled ETH across a curated set of validator operators that StakePool has vetted and contracted with. StakePool charges a 10% fee on staking rewards. spETH is freely transferable and trades on secondary markets. The protocol's governance is managed by a small team; token holders have limited governance rights. StakePool employs engineers to monitor validator performance and manages relationships with validator operators.

Analysis — This Is Where It Gets Interesting

FC-1 (Purpose): StakePool's purpose is to generate staking yield for its depositors — this looks like investment management. However, the protocol also has operational dimensions: vetting and managing validator relationships, engineering the smart contract infrastructure, monitoring performance. Is this investment management or operations? Judgment required. If the primary value creation is selecting and allocating to the best validators for yield optimization, this tilts toward investment management. If the primary value is the technical infrastructure, it tilts operational.

FC-2 (Mgmt Services): StakePool does make decisions on behalf of depositors about which validators to use — this is arguably investment management. However, the depositors didn't contract with StakePool for "investment management services" in the traditional sense — they used a protocol. This is a genuine grey area.

TC-4 (Equity Interests): spETH tokens represent residual claims on the pool's assets — this looks like equity. If spETH is classified as equity by the issuer, TC-4 is met.

TC-6 (FV Basis): The pool's value is determinable by the fair value of staked ETH. If the protocol tracks NAV and reports to depositors on a FV basis, TC-6 is met.

Investment Company Argument (If Structured Properly)

If StakePool: (1) treats its primary function as investment allocation (choosing validators = selecting investments), (2) reports pool value at FV, (3) has clear investment mandate, and (4) the operational activities (smart contracts, monitoring) are incidental to the investment function — then an investment company argument is viable. Structural separation of the protocol operator from the investment pool helps significantly.

Not an Investment Company Argument

If StakePool: (1) earns material revenue from protocol fees that are operational in nature, (2) has substantial engineering/operational headcount, (3) is governed primarily by tech/product roadmap decisions, and (4) the staking is fundamentally providing infrastructure as a service — then the operational character dominates. Consider whether a software company analogy is more apt than a fund analogy.

Conclusion: Judgment-Intensive — Likely Requires Legal Structuring Decision. The outcome depends heavily on how the entity is structured, governed, and described in its documents. Many similar protocols have pursued non-investment-company accounting by characterizing their activities as operational.
Scenario 3
NodeOps Inc. — A professional validator node operator
▶
Facts

NodeOps operates Ethereum, Solana, and Cosmos validator nodes on behalf of institutional clients. Clients delegate their tokens to NodeOps validators and pay a 5% commission on staking rewards. NodeOps runs the infrastructure, manages uptime, absorbs slashing insurance costs, and optimizes MEV. NodeOps also holds some ETH and SOL on its own balance sheet which is staked through its own validators. NodeOps' primary revenue is validator service commissions, not investment returns on its own holdings.

Assessment — Not an Investment Company

FC-1 FAILS: NodeOps' primary purpose is to provide validator infrastructure services, not to invest capital for investment returns. Its value proposition is operational excellence in running blockchain infrastructure. Its own holdings are incidental to this service business.

FC-2 FAILS: NodeOps provides infrastructure/validation services to its clients — not investment management services. Clients manage their own investment decisions about what to stake; NodeOps handles the technical execution. This is more analogous to an outsourced IT provider or prime broker than an investment manager.

Multiple TCs would also fail: The "clients" are not investors with equity interests in a fund — they are service customers. No pooling, no equity interests issued, no NAV-based management.

Conclusion: NOT an Investment Company. NodeOps is an operating company providing blockchain infrastructure services. Accounting follows general GAAP, not ASC 946. Own crypto holdings would be subject to ASU 2023-08 (FV option for in-scope assets) or ASC 350 (cost less impairment for intangibles, unless FV election made).
Scenario 4
DigitalHoldings Corp — Corporate treasury strategy investing heavily in Bitcoin
▶
Facts

DigitalHoldings Corp is a publicly traded company that converted substantially all of its corporate treasury to Bitcoin. The company has minimal operating revenue — it does occasional consulting work. Management has stated that BTC appreciation is the primary driver of shareholder value. The company holds $3B in BTC and has $5M in consulting revenue. The company's CEO regularly discusses BTC price targets on earnings calls. The company has issued equity (stock) that trades on the NYSE, and shareholders are implicitly "invested" in BTC appreciation through the stock.

Analysis — The Notorious Grey Area

FC-1: This is fact-intensive. The company's economic purpose has effectively become holding BTC for appreciation — which looks like investment activity. However, the legal form is a corporation with operating activities, not an investment fund. FASB has generally not blessed the reclassification of operating companies as investment companies simply because they hold large investment positions.

FC-2: The company is NOT providing investment management services to its shareholders. Shareholders buy stock through the public markets — they are not contributing capital to a managed fund. The company is managing its own balance sheet, not investors' portfolios.

TC Analysis: No pooling. No equity interests issued in a fund structure. Shareholders hold corporate equity, not fund interests. The company is not managing investments on behalf of investors — it is managing its own assets.

Conclusion: NOT an Investment Company — despite the economic reality looking investment-like. FC-2 is not met because the company is not providing investment management services to investors. Its BTC holdings are subject to ASU 2023-08 fair value accounting (as an elected option for in-scope crypto assets) or ASC 350. This conclusion is consistent with SEC staff positions as well.
