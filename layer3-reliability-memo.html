SECTION I Purpose of This Memorandum

This memorandum documents the engagement team's assessment of whether information obtained from Layer3 (layer3.xyz) meets the reliability standards required for use as audit evidence under:

  • Generally Accepted Auditing Standards (GAAS) as codified in the AICPA's Statements on Auditing Standards, specifically AU-C Section 500 as updated by SAS No. 142 (Audit Evidence, effective December 15, 2022); and
  • PCAOB Auditing Standards, specifically AS 1105 (Audit Evidence), including the amendments effective for fiscal years beginning on or after December 15, 2025, and the related PCAOB Staff Guidance issued October 2025 on evaluating the reliability of external electronic information.

This memo also addresses applicability of AU-C 402 / PCAOB AS 2601 (Audit Considerations Relating to an Entity Using a Service Organization) to the extent Layer3 may constitute a service organization relevant to an audit client's processing of transactions.

ⓘ
Scope Notice

This memorandum does not constitute a final opinion on the sufficiency of audit evidence in any specific engagement. Rather, it establishes a documented, risk-based framework for evaluating Layer3 data in the context of a particular audit client's facts and circumstances. Engagement-specific addenda are required for each use case.

SECTION II Background: What Is Layer3 and How Does It Work
2.1 Company Overview

Layer3 is a Web3 user acquisition and engagement platform founded in 2021 and headquartered in Washington, D.C. In functional terms, it is a marketing and distribution infrastructure tool used by blockchain-based companies to attract users, distribute tokens, and incentivize specific on-chain behaviors.

💬
Plain English

Think of Layer3 as a loyalty rewards program for the blockchain world — except instead of airline miles, users earn cryptocurrency tokens or digital credentials by completing tasks (called "Quests"). A blockchain company that wants to grow its user base pays Layer3 to run a campaign: users who interact with the company's product earn a reward, and Layer3 facilitates and verifies those interactions.

Notable clients: Coinbase Cloud, Aave, Arbitrum, Optimism, Uniswap, Base, 1inch, and Robinhood's blockchain operations. The platform has raised approximately $26.8 million across four funding rounds (Series A in May 2024; $4 million round in February 2025) from institutional investors including Electric Capital, Greenfield Capital, Amber Group, and ParaFi Capital.

Why this matters to an auditor: An audit client may have used Layer3 to distribute tokens as part of a token launch or airdrop, record proof of user activity on their blockchain protocol, or generate verifiable records of on-chain completions. The auditor may need to rely on Layer3 data to verify token distribution records, user counts, or on-chain activity volumes.

2.2 Technical Architecture — On-Chain vs. Off-Chain Data

This distinction is critical for audit purposes. Layer3 operates across two data environments with materially different reliability characteristics.

A. Off-Chain Data (Layer3's Internal Platform)
💻
Technical Definition

Off-chain data refers to information created, stored, and processed within Layer3's own centralized servers — not on a public blockchain. This is analogous to data housed in any SaaS company's private database.

Off-chain data includes: quest completion determinations (pre-minting logic), user account and identity records, campaign configuration data, and aggregate platform reporting dashboards. Audit implication: This data is subject to the same risks as any other third-party data — it can be modified, is privately stored, and has no independent public verification mechanism.

B. On-Chain Data (Blockchain-Recorded)
⛓
Technical Definition

On-chain data refers to information permanently recorded on a public blockchain through a confirmed network transaction. Once recorded, blockchain data is cryptographically immutable — it cannot be altered without the consensus of the entire distributed network, making tampering computationally infeasible.

💬
Plain English

Once Layer3 records something on the blockchain, it becomes a permanent, publicly verifiable entry in a distributed ledger that no single party — not even Layer3 — can change. Think of it like a permanent, tamper-proof public notarization recorded in a ledger shared by thousands of independent computers worldwide.

2.3 The CUBE Credential System

CUBE stands for Credentials to Unify Blockchain Events. CUBEs are ERC-721 tokens — a standardized type of non-fungible digital record on the Ethereum blockchain and compatible networks.

💬
Plain English for CPAs

An ERC-721 token is like a unique, serial-numbered digital certificate. Each one is distinct (non-fungible means no two are identical). When a user completes a Quest, Layer3 mints (creates and permanently records) a CUBE to that user's blockchain wallet address — capturing a timestamp, the wallet address, and metadata describing what was completed.

Data Contained in Each CUBE
  • Participating blockchain project(s) and communities involved
  • Quest difficulty level (beginner, intermediate, advanced)
  • Quest title and description
  • Timestamp of completion (blockchain-confirmed)
  • Recipient wallet address
  • Rich metadata stored on IPFS (InterPlanetary File System — a distributed, content-addressed storage protocol)
Smart Contract Architecture

Layer3's reward distribution system operates through three primary smart contracts:

  • CUBE.sol — mints and manages CUBE credentials
  • Factory.sol — creates and manages individual reward campaigns
  • Escrow.sol — holds token rewards and releases them upon verified quest completion
💬
Plain English

A smart contract is a self-executing program stored on the blockchain that automatically performs actions when predefined conditions are met — without human intervention or override capability after deployment. Think of it like a vending machine: put in the right input, get the promised output, every time, with no cashier who could decide otherwise.

Smart Contract Audit & Open-Source Status

In July 2024, Layer3's smart contracts were independently audited by ThreeSigma, a professional blockchain security firm. The contracts are also open source (published on GitHub under the Apache 2.0 license), enabling independent code review by the auditor's specialists. Layer3 also maintains an active bug bounty program with a dedicated security contact.

Cryptographic Verification — EIP-712 Signatures
💬
Plain English

EIP-712 is a standardized cryptographic signature protocol that binds specific structured data to a specific authorized party — like a tamper-evident wax seal. If anything in the underlying data changes after the signature is applied, the seal breaks, making any modification immediately detectable. This creates a verifiable chain of custody from data creation to on-chain recording.

SECTION III Applicable Auditing Standards and Reliability Requirements
3.1 GAAS — AICPA AU-C 500 / SAS No. 142 (Non-Public Company Audits)

SAS No. 142 (Audit Evidence, effective December 15, 2022) replaced the prior AU-C 500 and established a modernized, principles-based framework for evaluating audit evidence. Under SAS 142, the auditor must evaluate evidence for sufficiency (quantity) and appropriateness (relevance and reliability).

For external information sources specifically, the standard requires consideration of the following reliability factors:

Factor Standard's Requirement Plain English
AccuracyIs the information factually correct? Does it reflect what it purports to reflect?Is the data right?
CompletenessDoes it capture all relevant items within the population?Does it include everything it should?
AuthenticityIs it genuine and from the purported source? Has it been altered?Is this really from Layer3, and has no one tampered with it?
Susceptibility to BiasCan the source or audit client influence or alter the information?Can anyone manipulate this data?
Controls over ProductionWhat controls govern how the information is created, maintained, and transmitted?What's stopping errors or fraud from entering the data?
Source IndependenceIs the source independent of management and the audit client?Did a truly outside party produce this, free from client influence?

SAS 142 specifically notes that external information is more likely to be reliable when it is provided to the public without restriction or to a broad range of users for a fee — because such information is less subject to influence by any single interested party.

3.2 GAAS — AU-C 402: Audit Considerations Relating to an Entity Using a Service Organization

If the audit client outsources a function relevant to financial reporting to Layer3 (e.g., Layer3 processes and records token distributions that flow into the client's financial records), then Layer3 may constitute a service organization under AU-C 402.

💬
Plain English

AU-C 402 applies when your client hands off part of their accounting or transaction processing to a third party. If that third party's activities affect the financial statements, you need to understand what controls exist there — you cannot simply ignore it.

Under AU-C 402, the auditor must:

  1. Obtain an understanding of the services Layer3 provides and their relevance to the client's internal controls over financial reporting
  2. Determine whether sufficient appropriate evidence can be obtained regarding controls at Layer3 without a direct visit
  3. The preferred mechanism is a SOC 1 Type 2 report — an independent CPA firm's examination of the service organization's controls over a defined period
  4. If a SOC 1 report is unavailable, the auditor must perform substitute procedures: direct inquiry, inspection of SLAs, or direct observation of controls
3.3 PCAOB AS 1105 — Audit Evidence (Public Company Audits)

AS 1105 is the PCAOB's counterpart to AU-C 500 and governs evidence in audits of issuers. The standard requires that evidence be both relevant and reliable. Key existing guidance provides that evidence from independent external sources is more reliable than internally generated evidence, and that evidence obtained directly by the auditor is more reliable than evidence obtained indirectly. If a third party provides evidence subject to restrictions, limitations, or disclaimers, the auditor must evaluate those limitations on reliability.

⚠
New Amendment — AS 1105 Paragraph .10A (Effective December 15, 2025)

The PCAOB adopted amendments to AS 1105 in June 2024, effective for fiscal years beginning on or after December 15, 2025. Paragraph .10A directly and specifically addresses external electronic information provided by the company to the auditor.

Under paragraph .10A, when a company provides the auditor with external electronic information (e.g., a Layer3 data export provided by management as support for a financial statement assertion), the auditor must:

  • (a) Obtain an understanding of: the source of the information (Layer3 — who they are, what they do, how data is generated) and the company's process for receiving, maintaining, and processing that information (how the client receives and handles Layer3 data before providing it to the auditor)
  • (b) Test either: the information itself (for accuracy, completeness, and authenticity), or the relevant controls (the controls that the client and/or Layer3 have in place to ensure the information is accurate and complete)
ⓘ
The "Remote Possibility" Exception (PCAOB Board Policy Statement, September 2025)

The PCAOB clarified that if the auditor concludes there is no more than a remote possibility that the information has been modified in a way that renders it unreliable, then separate testing under .10A(b) is not required. This exception is most applicable where the auditor independently obtains data from a public, tamper-proof source — such as directly querying a public blockchain.

💬
Plain English

Paragraph .10A says: you cannot simply take the client's word for it that a Layer3 report is accurate. You need to understand where the data came from and either test the data itself or test the controls that produced it. If you pull blockchain data directly from a public explorer yourself, you may be able to skip separate testing — because the blockchain is independently verifiable and cannot be altered.

3.4 PCAOB AS 2601 — Consideration of an Entity's Use of a Service Organization

The PCAOB equivalent of AU-C 402. Under AS 2601, if Layer3 processes transactions on behalf of the audit client in a way relevant to the financial statements, the auditor must:

  • Obtain an understanding of Layer3's services and the client's controls over those services
  • Evaluate whether the services create risks of material misstatement
  • Determine how to obtain sufficient evidence — via a service auditor's report (SOC 1) or direct procedures at Layer3
  • For integrated audits (ICFR under AS 2201): consider whether controls at Layer3 are part of the client's ICFR and, if so, obtain evidence on their operating effectiveness

4.2 On-Chain Data — Accuracy, Completeness, and Authenticity
Overall Assessment: ● High for data verifiable directly on the blockchain

This is Layer3's most significant reliability strength. Data recorded on a public blockchain has the following properties:

  • Immutability: Once a CUBE credential or token distribution is recorded on-chain, it cannot be altered, deleted, or retroactively modified by Layer3, the client, or any other party without the cryptographic consensus of the entire network — which is computationally infeasible.
  • Public verifiability: All on-chain transactions are visible on public blockchain explorers (Basescan for Base Network, Etherscan for Ethereum). The auditor or specialist can independently query the blockchain to verify CUBE mints, token distributions, wallet addresses, and timestamps — without relying on Layer3 to provide or certify the data.
  • Smart contract integrity: Contracts are open source (GitHub, Apache 2.0), independently audited by ThreeSigma (July 2024), and governed by EIP-712 cryptographic signatures.
  • IPFS metadata integrity: Content-addressed storage means the file's address IS its cryptographic hash — if the file changes, the address changes, making undetected alteration infeasible.
💬
Plain English

If the client says "Layer3 distributed 1 million tokens to 50,000 wallets," the auditor can directly verify this by querying the blockchain — a public ledger that no one controls. It is like checking a public county deed record rather than taking the real estate agent's word for it.

⚠
Limitation — The Oracle Problem

The reliability of on-chain data is undermined when off-chain information is used to trigger on-chain events — known as the Oracle Problem (documented in the Journal of Information Systems, American Accounting Association, 2021, and AICPA digital asset guidance). Layer3 must first determine off-chain whether a user completed a Quest before minting a CUBE on-chain. If Layer3's off-chain verification logic is flawed or manipulable, even the immutable on-chain record it produces may not accurately reflect the real-world event it purports to capture.

4.3 Off-Chain Data — Accuracy, Completeness, and Authenticity
Overall Assessment: ● Requires Additional Procedures
Reliability FactorFinding
AccuracyCannot be independently verified without access to Layer3's internal systems or a third-party attestation report.
CompletenessNo public mechanism to confirm all records are captured — depends entirely on Layer3's internal data practices.
AuthenticityCannot confirm that data provided by the client (obtained from Layer3) has not been filtered, modified, or selectively presented without Layer3's direct corroboration.
Susceptibility to BiasLayer3 clients configure campaigns and define success criteria. Client-influenced data presentation cannot be ruled out without understanding Layer3's data segregation and reporting integrity controls.
Controls over ProductionNo SOC 1 or SOC 2 report identified as of the date of this memorandum. Absent such a report, the auditor has no independent attestation of Layer3's internal control environment.
⛔
Critical Gap — No SOC Report Identified

As of April 25, 2026, no evidence of a SOC 1 (Type 1 or Type 2) or SOC 2 report for Layer3 has been identified through research. A SOC 1 Type 2 report is the standard mechanism by which auditors obtain assurance over a service organization's control environment under AU-C 402 and PCAOB AS 2601. The absence of a SOC report does not render Layer3 unreliable as a platform, but it means the auditor cannot rely on Layer3's attestation of its own controls as audit evidence — additional direct procedures are required.

💬
Plain English

A SOC report is like a health inspection certificate posted in a restaurant window. It tells you an independent third party came in, examined how the kitchen operates, and confirmed the controls are working. Without a SOC report, you have to inspect the kitchen yourself — which means more work for the auditor.

4.4 Independence of the Source from Management
Overall Assessment: ● Partial — Depends on Data Type
  • On-chain data queried directly from the blockchain: Fully independent of both Layer3 management and audit client management. The blockchain is maintained by a decentralized network of validators — no single party controls it.
  • On-chain data obtained via Layer3 platform export (client-provided): Less independent — the data has passed through the client's hands before reaching the auditor. The auditor should independently re-verify against the blockchain explorer.
  • Off-chain data provided by Layer3 to the client: Partially independent of the client, but not independent of Layer3. Layer3's data reflects Layer3's own records, which cannot be independently corroborated without direct inquiry at Layer3 or a SOC report.
4.5 Susceptibility to Management Bias
On-Chain: ● Low Risk   Off-Chain: ● Moderate Risk
  • On-chain CUBE data: Extremely low susceptibility. Once minted on the blockchain, the record is immutable and cannot be altered by the client or Layer3.
  • Off-chain campaign and platform data: Moderate susceptibility. Campaign parameters (which actions count as Quest completions) are configured by Layer3's clients. These parameters could theoretically be engineered to produce favorable metrics. The auditor should understand who controls campaign configuration and what segregation of duties exists.
4.6 Restrictions, Limitations, and Disclaimers
Assessment: ● Evaluate Carefully

Under PCAOB AS 1105, the auditor must evaluate the impact of any restrictions, limitations, or disclaimers attached to third-party information. The engagement team should review:

  • Terms of Service / API Terms: Confirm whether Layer3's terms disclaim accuracy or completeness of the data, limit liability for errors, or restrict use for attestation purposes.
  • Data export formats: Confirm whether data exports are provided with any caveats regarding completeness or currency.
  • Service Level Agreements (SLAs): Review SLAs between the audit client and Layer3 to understand what data accuracy and availability guarantees, if any, Layer3 has committed to.
💬
Plain English

If the fine print on Layer3's platform says "we make no guarantees about the accuracy of our data," that disclaimer directly affects how much weight the auditor can put on that data. Read the terms.

SECTION VI Required Procedures to Support Reliance on Layer3 Data

The following procedures are required (or strongly recommended) when relying on Layer3 data as audit evidence. All procedures should be documented in the engagement workpapers.

For All Engagements — GAAS and PCAOB
  • 1
    Understand the Service

    Document the specific services Layer3 provides to the client and how those services relate to financial statement assertions (existence, completeness, accuracy, cutoff, valuation, rights and obligations, presentation and disclosure).

  • 2
    Review Governing Documents

    Obtain and review the Master Service Agreement, Terms of Service, and any API agreements between the client and Layer3. Document any disclaimers or limitations that affect data reliability.

  • 3
    Assess Relevance

    Determine which specific Layer3-sourced data fields or reports are being used to support which specific financial statement line items or disclosures.

  • 4
    Obtain Written Representations

    If relying on off-chain Layer3 data, consider obtaining a management representation letter from the client affirming the accuracy and completeness of data obtained from Layer3. For material items, consider directly contacting Layer3 for corroboration (analogous to an external confirmation under AU-C 505).

For On-Chain Data
  • 5
    Independent Blockchain Verification

    Directly query the relevant public blockchain (Base, Ethereum) using a blockchain explorer (Basescan, Etherscan) or blockchain analytics tool. Document the query parameters, source URL, query date, and results. This step converts the evidence from "client-provided external data" to "auditor-obtained direct evidence" — materially improving its reliability classification under both AS 1105 and SAS 142.

  • 6
    Engage a Blockchain Specialist (AU-C 620 / PCAOB AS 1201)

    For material balances or disclosures dependent on Layer3 on-chain data, engage a specialist with blockchain expertise. Document the specialist's qualifications, the nature of the work performed, and the auditor's evaluation of the specialist's findings.

  • 7
    Evaluate the Oracle Problem

    For each Quest type where Layer3 determines off-chain whether an action was completed before minting a CUBE on-chain, obtain an understanding of Layer3's verification logic. Document the risk that the on-chain record may not accurately reflect the real-world event it purports to capture, and design procedures to address this risk.

  • 8
    Test Smart Contract Integrity

    Confirm that the smart contracts currently deployed match the open-source code on GitHub and the version reviewed in the ThreeSigma July 2024 audit. Note any post-audit upgrades and evaluate their significance on the reliability assessment.

For Off-Chain Data (If Required)
  • 9
    SOC Report Inquiry

    Inquire of Layer3 whether a SOC 1 or SOC 2 report is available. If one has been issued since the date of this memo, obtain and evaluate it under AU-C 402 / AS 2601. Update this reliability assessment accordingly.

  • 10
    Substitute Procedures (Absent SOC Report)

    In the absence of a SOC report, perform one or more of: (a) direct inquiry to Layer3 about internal controls over data accuracy, completeness, and security; (b) inspection of Layer3's system security certifications (e.g., ISO 27001); (c) testing a sample of off-chain records against corresponding on-chain data for consistency; (d) confirmation of specific data points directly with Layer3 (analogous to an external confirmation).

PCAOB-Specific — AS 1105 Paragraph .10A
  • 11
    Document the Source Understanding (.10A(a))

    Prepare a written description of Layer3's business, data generation process, and the client's process for receiving, maintaining, and processing Layer3 data — satisfying the understanding requirement of .10A(a).

  • 12
    Test Information or Controls (.10A(b))

    For material items, select and execute one of: Test the information (trace a sample of Layer3 records to on-chain blockchain records; reconcile totals; test completeness against event logs) or Test the controls (test the client's controls over receipt and processing of Layer3 data: input validation, reconciliation procedures, access controls).

  • 13
    Evaluate the Remote Possibility Exception

    If the auditor independently obtains on-chain data directly from a public blockchain explorer (not through Layer3 or the client), document the basis for concluding there is no more than a remote possibility the data has been modified — and that separate .10A(b) testing is accordingly not required for that specific data set.

SECTION VIII Gaps and Caveats
  • ⚠
    No SOC 1 or SOC 2 report identified as of April 25, 2026. This is the most significant gap in Layer3's audit reliability profile. The engagement team should re-inquire prior to each engagement period in case a report has since been issued.
  • ⚠
    Oracle Problem is inherent and irreducible for off-chain Quest verification — the auditor must design specific procedures to address this risk for any material balance dependent on Layer3's off-chain determination logic.
  • ⚠
    Smart contract audit (ThreeSigma, July 2024) is now approximately 21 months old. Confirm whether the audited contracts remain unchanged and that no material upgrades have been deployed to the on-chain contracts since that audit.
  • ⚠
    Layer3 is a relatively young company with limited operating history. Assess whether the risk profile remains appropriate as the engagement evolves and as the platform's controls environment matures.
  • ⚠
    This memo is a general framework. It must be supplemented with engagement-specific procedures tailored to the specific financial statement assertions being supported by Layer3 data, the materiality of those amounts, and the client's specific use case on the Layer3 platform.
  • ⚠
    Regulatory environment for blockchain platforms is evolving rapidly. Monitor for new AICPA, PCAOB, or SEC guidance on auditing digital asset-related service organizations that may update this analysis.
SECTION IX Key Takeaways
  • ✔
    On-chain Layer3 data can be used as audit evidence — it is immutable, publicly verifiable, and independently confirmable. The auditor must directly query the blockchain rather than relying solely on client-provided exports.
  • ✔
    Off-chain Layer3 data requires additional procedures — specifically, the procedures that would normally be satisfied by a SOC report, which does not currently exist for Layer3.
  • ✔
    PCAOB AS 1105 paragraph .10A now requires that auditors formally document their understanding of Layer3 as a source and either test the data or the controls — this is a new mandatory step for PCAOB engagements beginning after December 15, 2025.
  • ✔
    A blockchain specialist must be engaged under AU-C 620 / AS 1201 for any material balance or disclosure that depends on Layer3's smart contract or on-chain data — the technical complexity exceeds the expertise expected of a general practitioner CPA.
  • ✔
    Absence of a SOC report is the most significant known gap — the engagement team must obtain substitute assurance through direct inquiry, documentation review, and independent blockchain verification procedures.
