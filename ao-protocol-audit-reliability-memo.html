SECTION I Purpose of This Memorandum

This memorandum documents the engagement team's assessment of whether information generated by the AO Protocol (Actor-Oriented) — a decentralized computation network built on the Arweave permanent storage blockchain — meets the reliability standards required for use as audit evidence under:

  • Generally Accepted Auditing Standards (GAAS) as codified in the AICPA's Statements on Auditing Standards, specifically AU-C Section 500 as updated by SAS No. 142 (Audit Evidence, effective December 15, 2022); and
  • PCAOB Auditing Standards, specifically AS 1105 (Audit Evidence), including amendments effective for fiscal years beginning on or after December 15, 2025, and the related PCAOB Staff Guidance issued October 2025 on evaluating the reliability of external electronic information.

An audit client may hold assets denominated in AO tokens, operate financial applications deployed as AO processes (smart contracts), or have contractual obligations recorded on the AO network. This memorandum establishes the framework for determining whether on-chain AO data is sufficiently reliable to serve as audit evidence for those assertions.

ⓘ
Scope Notice

This memorandum does not constitute a final opinion on the sufficiency of audit evidence in any specific engagement. It establishes a documented, risk-based framework for evaluating AO Protocol data in the context of a particular audit client's facts and circumstances. Engagement-specific addenda are required for each use case.

SECTION II Background: What Is AO Protocol and How Does It Work
2.1 Protocol Overview

AO (Actor-Oriented) is a decentralized computation network built on top of the Arweave blockchain. It was developed by the Arweave team, announced in February 2024, and launched on mainnet on February 8, 2025. AO allows developers to deploy programs called processes — analogous to smart contracts — that run in parallel with no practical computational size limits. Prior to mainnet launch, the AO testnet processed over 1.5 billion messages with more than $700 million pre-bridged by participants.

💬
Plain English for CPAs

Think of AO as a decentralized cloud computer with a permanent, tamper-proof filing cabinet built in. Programs run on the computer, and every input those programs ever received is stored permanently in the filing cabinet — publicly accessible to anyone, forever. No single company owns or controls either the computer or the filing cabinet.

2.2 The Two-Layer Architecture — Critical Distinction for Auditors

AO operates across two distinct layers with materially different reliability characteristics. Understanding this distinction is essential before evaluating any specific piece of AO-derived audit evidence.

LayerWhat It DoesAudit AnalogyImmutability
Arweave Base LayerPermanently stores every message ever sent to every AO process — the historical ledger of all inputsA filing cabinet that can never be altered or destroyedPermanent — economically incentivized to persist 200+ years
AO Compute LayerProcesses messages, executes process (smart contract) logic, and produces outputs and stateThe accounting system that processes transactions and produces balancesDerived — computed deterministically from permanent message logs; independently reproducible by anyone
ⓘ
Key Audit Implication

The inputs (messages) are permanent and immutable. The outputs (process state / balances) are computed deterministically from those inputs and can be independently verified by any party replaying the same message history through the same process logic. This creates an audit trail that is structurally more transparent than traditional enterprise software.

2.3 AO Processes — What Runs on AO

AO hosts processes — programs deployed by developers that execute logic in response to incoming messages. Processes are the AO equivalent of smart contracts on Ethereum. The primary programming language is Lua, a lightweight scripting language, though AO also supports EVM (Ethereum Virtual Machine), SVM (Solana Virtual Machine), and WASM (WebAssembly) environments. Each process maintains its own independent state — there is no shared global state between processes, which is a significant architectural departure from traditional blockchains.

Financial applications that an audit client may operate on AO include: decentralized exchanges, token minting and distribution programs, autonomous yield protocols, lending and borrowing protocols, and treasury management systems.

⚠
Important Limitation

AO's integrity guarantees apply to the data flowing through processes — not to the correctness of the logic written into those processes. A smart contract with a coding error will execute that error immutably and permanently. Auditors must separately assess both data integrity (addressed by AO's architecture) and code correctness (requires specialist review). These are independent questions requiring different procedures.

SECTION III Technical Architecture — Node Types, Message Flow, and Storage
3.1 The Three Node Types

AO's network is operated by three specialized node types. Each plays a distinct role in the message lifecycle, and understanding their functions is essential for assessing where reliability strengths and weaknesses exist.

Node TypePrimary FunctionAudit RelevanceTrust Level
Messenger Unit (MU) Gateway between users and the network. Authenticates digital signatures on incoming messages before routing. Returns computed results to the caller. First point of cryptographic authentication — prevents unsigned or forged messages from entering the system Multiple MUs exist; no single point of failure; authentication is cryptographic, not discretionary
Scheduler Unit (SU) Assigns each message a unique, atomically incrementing nonce within its process — analogous to Ethereum's nonce concept. Uploads the ordered message to Arweave to establish permanent sequence. Critical for audit trail integrity — establishes the canonical, tamper-proof order of all events. The SU's Arweave upload is the moment an event becomes permanent, immutable audit evidence. High — once written to Arweave, the sequence cannot be altered without re-mining the entire subsequent chain
Compute Unit (CU) Fetches the complete ordered message history from Arweave, runs the process logic, computes the resulting state, and returns a cryptographically signed proof of the computation. Source of financial state outputs (balances, transfers, contract results). CUs compete in an open market; multiple CUs can compute and sign the same state independently. Medium — CUs are competitive nodes; determinism ensures honest CUs agree, but no protocol-level consensus validates outputs. Multi-CU confirmation recommended for material balances.
3.2 Arweave's Underlying Consensus — The Permanent Storage Layer

Arweave uses a unique data structure called the blockweave. Unlike traditional blockchains where each block links only to the immediately preceding block, each Arweave block links to two prior blocks: the immediately preceding block and a randomly selected block from the network's full history (the recall block). To mine a new block, a miner must have access to the recall block — creating a direct economic incentive to store the entire historical dataset, not just recent data.

💬
Plain English

Traditional hard drives are wiped and reused. Arweave's system economically rewards miners for never deleting anything — because the more old data you store, the better your chances of winning the next block reward. This is why AO's audit trail is structurally more durable than data stored in cloud services that can be deleted, cancelled, or go out of business.

Arweave's consensus mechanism is called Succinct Proofs of Random Access (SPoRA), combined with a Verifiable Delay Function (VDF) — a cryptographic speed limit that ensures mining is bottlenecked by storage capacity, not computing speed. The economic model funds data permanence through a one-time upfront fee paid by data uploaders into a storage endowment, designed to sustain miner incentives for an estimated 200+ years.

4.2 The Holographic State — Independent Reconstructibility

AO introduces a concept called holographic state: because every message that ever changed a process's state is permanently stored on Arweave in order, the current (or any historical) state of any process can be reconstructed by any party at any time by replaying the complete message history through the process logic. No single node needs to be online or trusted.

💬
Practitioner Illustration

Suppose a client holds 50,000 AO tokens in a smart contract process as of the audit date. On a traditional blockchain, the auditor typically queries the current balance and accepts that output. On AO, the auditor can instead obtain the complete ordered message log for that process from Arweave (public and permanent), replay every message through the same contract logic, and arrive at the same balance independently — without asking the client or the protocol's nodes for anything. The result is an independently derived balance, analogous to a bank confirmation that does not require the bank's cooperation.

⚠
What Immutability Does Not Mean

Immutability of the message log does not mean the process logic is correct or behaves as intended. A smart contract with a coding error executes that error immutably and permanently. The auditor's procedures must assess both (1) the integrity of the data — addressed by the mechanisms above — and (2) the correctness of the program logic — which requires a separate specialist review. These are independent questions requiring different procedures.

SECTION V Applicable Auditing Standards and Reliability Requirements
5.1 GAAS — AICPA AU-C 500 / SAS No. 142

SAS No. 142 (Audit Evidence, effective December 15, 2022) established a modernized framework for evaluating audit evidence. The standard requires consideration of sufficiency (quantity) and appropriateness (relevance and reliability). For external information sources, the following reliability factors apply:

FactorStandard's RequirementPlain English
AccuracyIs the information factually correct and does it reflect what it purports to reflect?Is the data right?
CompletenessDoes it capture all relevant items within the population?Does it include everything it should?
AuthenticityIs it genuine and from the purported source? Has it been altered?Is this really from the AO network, and has no one tampered with it?
Susceptibility to BiasCan the source or audit client influence or alter the information?Can anyone manipulate this data?
Controls over ProductionWhat controls govern how the information is created, maintained, and transmitted?What prevents errors or fraud from entering the data?
Source IndependenceIs the source independent of management and the audit client?Did a truly outside party produce this, free from client influence?
5.2 PCAOB AS 1105 — Audit Evidence (Public Company Audits)

AS 1105 requires that evidence be both relevant and reliable. Evidence from independent external sources is more reliable than internally generated evidence; evidence obtained directly by the auditor is more reliable than evidence obtained indirectly.

⚠
New Amendment — AS 1105 Paragraph .10A (Effective December 15, 2025)

Paragraph .10A directly addresses external electronic information provided by the company to the auditor. When a client provides AO-derived data (e.g., a process state export as support for a balance assertion), the auditor must: (a) obtain an understanding of the source of the information and the company's process for receiving and maintaining it; and (b) test either the information itself or the relevant controls that produced it.

ⓘ
The "Remote Possibility" Exception (PCAOB Board Policy Statement, September 2025)

The PCAOB clarified that if the auditor concludes there is no more than a remote possibility that the information has been modified in a way that renders it unreliable, separate testing under .10A(b) is not required. This exception is applicable where the auditor independently retrieves and replays AO message logs from Arweave's public, tamper-proof storage — since those logs cannot be altered post-settlement.

💬
Plain English

You cannot simply take the client's word that an AO process balance is accurate. You need to either test the data yourself or test the controls that produced it. If you pull message logs directly from Arweave and independently replay them, you may be able to skip separate testing — because those logs are publicly accessible and mathematically impossible to alter after settlement.

SECTION VI Evaluation of AO Protocol Against Audit Reliability Standards
6.1 Source Independence
Overall Assessment: ● High

Arweave's permanent message logs are public, decentralized, and not controlled by the audit client, the protocol's developers, or any single party. Any auditor can access them independently without the client's assistance. AO network node operators have no relationship with or incentive to assist any particular audit client. This is structurally stronger independence than most third-party confirmations, which require the confirming party's cooperation.

6.2 Data Accuracy and Authenticity (On-Chain)
Overall Assessment: ● High for data independently retrieved from Arweave

Digital signatures authenticate every message at the MU layer. Sequential nonces establish tamper-proof ordering. Arweave's blockweave makes historical alteration computationally infeasible. The auditor who independently retrieves message logs from Arweave and replays them is working with raw, unadulterated inputs — not a client-filtered or vendor-filtered export.

6.3 Compute Unit Consensus and Output Verification
Overall Assessment: ● Moderate

Unlike traditional blockchains where validators reach network-wide consensus before accepting a state change, AO Compute Units are a competitive market. There is no protocol-level mechanism requiring all CUs to agree before a result is accepted. While determinism ensures any honest CU produces the same result, a CU producing incorrect or malicious output is a theoretical risk. Auditors should query multiple independent CUs and compare signed state proofs for material balances.

6.4 Protocol Maturity and Track Record
Overall Assessment: ● Moderate — Developing

AO mainnet launched February 8, 2025 — approximately 14 months prior to this memorandum. While the testnet demonstrated substantial scale (1.5B+ messages, $700M+ pre-bridged), mainnet exposure to adversarial conditions, production-scale attack vectors, and edge cases is limited relative to established financial infrastructure. This risk diminishes over time as the protocol matures. Auditors should re-evaluate this dimension annually.

6.5 Regulatory Recognition
Overall Assessment: ● Low-Moderate

AO Protocol is not currently referenced in any FASB, SEC, PCAOB, or AICPA guidance specific to this platform. General digital asset audit guidance (AICPA Digital Assets Practice Aid; FASB ASC 350-60 effective for fiscal years beginning after December 15, 2024) applies but does not address AO-specific characteristics. The absence of specific regulatory recognition is a gap that heightens inherent risk and requires additional compensating procedures.

SECTION VII Risk Matrix — Audit-Relevant Limitations and Gaps
  • ⚠
    HIGH — Smart Contract Code Risk (Logic Errors Execute Permanently)
    AO processes execute Lua (or EVM/WASM) code deterministically and permanently. A bug, intentional backdoor, or design flaw in the process logic is as immutable as correct code. There is no rollback mechanism at the protocol level. An error that misallocates tokens or miscalculates balances is recorded permanently. The auditor cannot assess the reliability of AO output without understanding — or engaging a specialist to assess — the correctness of the underlying process code.
  • ⚠
    HIGH — Compute Unit Market — No Consensus Validation of Output
    AO Compute Units are competitive market participants that independently compute and sign process states. There is no protocol-level mechanism requiring all CUs to agree before a result is accepted. Unlike traditional blockchains, no network-wide consensus validates state outputs. Auditors should query at least two independent CUs and compare signed results when the balance under audit is material.
  • ⚠
    HIGH — Protocol Immaturity — Limited Mainnet Track Record
    AO mainnet launched February 8, 2025. As of this memorandum's date, the protocol has approximately 14 months of mainnet operation. Standard auditing practice assigns higher inherent risk to systems without an extended production track record. This risk diminishes over time as the protocol matures.
  • ⚠
    MEDIUM — No Legal Enforceability Framework
    AO smart contract processes execute autonomously based on code logic. There is no legal framework specifically governing the enforceability of AO process outputs in most jurisdictions. Auditors must assess whether on-chain events have the legal substance required for the financial reporting treatment the client has applied, and whether supplemental off-chain documentation is needed.
  • ⚠
    MEDIUM — Pseudonymous Addresses — Identity Verification Gap
    AO transactions are identified by cryptographic wallet addresses, not legal entity names. The auditor can verify that wallet address X sent Y tokens to wallet address Z, but cannot from on-chain data alone verify the legal identity of the counterparties. Third-party confirmations or client-provided address attribution documentation is required to link on-chain events to financial reporting entities.
  • ⚠
    LOW — Historical Data Manipulation Risk
    Once messages are written to Arweave's blockweave, altering them would require re-mining every subsequent block — computationally infeasible given the network's hash power and VDF speed limit. Historical immutability of settled messages is assessed as very high. This risk is materially lower than for traditional enterprise database systems where historical records can be altered by database administrators.
  • ⚠
    LOW — Network Availability Risk
    AO's holographic state architecture means that even if all active AO nodes were offline, process history remains accessible through Arweave's decentralized storage. Process state can be reconstructed from Arweave data without requiring any AO-specific node to be operational — materially lower availability dependency than centralized cloud services.
SECTION VIII Recommended Audit Procedures
⚠
Specialist Engagement — AU-C 620 / PCAOB AS 1210

Procedures 2, 3, and 6 below may require engagement of a specialist. The auditor should assess whether the engagement team has sufficient competence in AO process inspection, Lua code review, and Arweave data retrieval, and engage an appropriately qualified IT specialist or blockchain forensics firm where that competence is absent.

Evidence Gathering
  • 1
    Obtain and Inspect AO Process Identifiers

    Obtain from the client the Process IDs for all AO processes material to the financial statements. Verify that client-provided Process IDs correspond to on-chain processes by independently querying the AO network or Arweave directly. Confirm the process creation transaction, the deployment wallet address, and the initializing code hash. Document the chain of custody between the client's legal entity and the wallet addresses controlling material processes.

  • 2
    Independently Retrieve and Replay Message Logs

    For material AO processes, independently retrieve the complete ordered message log from Arweave using the SU-assigned nonces as the ordering index. Replay the message log through the process logic to independently compute process state as of the audit date and at prior period-end dates. Compare independently computed states to client-reported balances. Any discrepancy is a significant finding requiring investigation. Document the tool or method used for replay and retain the evidence.

  • 3
    Engage Specialist for Smart Contract Code Review

    Engage a qualified specialist to review the process code (typically Lua) for: (a) logical correctness relative to the intended business function; (b) presence of admin keys, backdoors, or upgrade mechanisms that could allow unilateral state manipulation; (c) known vulnerability patterns applicable to the AO/Lua execution environment; and (d) any deviation between the deployed code and off-chain documentation of the intended process behavior. Obtain the specialist's written report and evaluate its findings in light of the audit objectives.

Corroboration and Confirmation
  • 4
    Multi-CU State Confirmation for Material Balances

    For balances material to the financial statements, query at least two independent Compute Units for the process state as of the audit date. Compare the signed state proofs returned by each CU. Consistent results across independent CUs corroborate the computed state. Discrepancies between CUs require investigation of the underlying message logs and computation to determine the cause.

  • 5
    Wallet Address Attribution Confirmation

    Obtain client-provided documentation attributing each material wallet address to a specific legal entity or individual. Verify attribution through corroborating evidence: signed attestation from the wallet controller, transaction history showing transfers between a verified exchange account and the wallet, or a challenge-response test where the client signs a message with the private key to prove control. This procedure addresses the identity gap inherent in pseudonymous on-chain systems.

Control and Completeness Assessment
  • 6
    Assess Admin Key and Upgrade Mechanism Risk

    Determine whether any AO process material to the financial statements contains admin keys, owner-only functions, or upgrade mechanisms that permit modification of process logic or state outside the normal user message pathway. If such mechanisms exist: (a) assess whether they have been used during the audit period by inspecting the message log for admin-function invocations; (b) assess segregation of duties over admin key custody; and (c) evaluate whether the existence of these mechanisms affects the reliability of on-chain data for audit purposes. Processes with active upgrade mechanisms should be treated with heightened skepticism.

  • 7
    Reconcile On-Chain Events to Financial Statement Presentation

    Trace material on-chain events identified in the message log to their corresponding entries in the client's general ledger and financial statements. Assess the appropriateness of the client's accounting classification (e.g., whether a token transfer has been correctly classified as a sale, exchange, or internal transfer). Evaluate whether the client has identified and disclosed all material on-chain activity, including any activity in wallets or processes not previously disclosed to the auditor.

9.2 Regulatory and Accounting Framework
⚠
SEC Registration Analysis

The AO token's distribution structure — 0% pre-sale, 0% pre-allocation, fully community-distributed — was deliberately designed to reduce securities law exposure. However, the Howey test analysis is fact-specific and no no-action letter or formal SEC guidance has been issued covering AO tokens specifically. Clients holding material AO token positions should obtain qualified securities law counsel regarding the applicable regulatory classification in their jurisdiction before financial statement issuance.

⚠
FASB ASC 350-60 — Digital Assets (Effective December 15, 2024)

For fiscal years beginning after December 15, 2024, FASB ASC 350-60 requires entities to measure certain digital assets at fair value through net income. AO tokens meeting the definition (fungible, on a distributed ledger, secured through cryptography) must be assessed under ASC 350-60. The auditor should confirm the client has appropriately identified all AO token holdings and selected an appropriate valuation methodology given the asset's trading history and market depth.

9.3 AO Processes as Financial Instruments

AO smart contract processes may create financial instruments, obligations, or revenue streams requiring separate financial reporting assessment. An AO process functioning as a decentralized lending protocol, for example, creates loan receivables and payables with specific accounting treatment requirements under ASC 310 or IFRS 9. The auditor must understand the economic substance of each AO process material to the financial statements — not merely the on-chain technical mechanics.

10.2 Key Takeaways
  • ✔AO Protocol data — specifically permanent message logs on Arweave and deterministically computed process states derived from them — constitutes reliable audit evidence with qualified conditions. Its core integrity characteristics are structurally superior to many traditional enterprise information systems.
  • ✔The holographic state is AO's most auditor-friendly feature: any party can independently reconstruct any historical process state by replaying the permanent, public Arweave message log — without the client's cooperation or a third-party confirmation.
  • ✔AO data should not be treated as stand-alone sufficient appropriate audit evidence without: (1) independent message log retrieval and replay; (2) specialist code review; and (3) identity attribution procedures linking wallet addresses to legal entities.
  • ✔The protocol's maturity limitation is temporal. As the mainnet track record extends and if no material protocol failures emerge, the reliability classification should be reassessed upward in subsequent audit periods. Re-evaluate annually.
  • ✔AO's data integrity guarantees apply to the data flowing through processes, not the logic written into them. Smart contract code review by a qualified specialist is always required separately and cannot be substituted by data integrity testing alone.
